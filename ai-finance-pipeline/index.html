Regional Snapshot

Across Asia-Pacific, employers are already competing for workers with AI skills.

This Power BI chart, built from the Our World in Data compilation of the Stanford AI Index, shows the average share of all job postings that mention AI-related skills — broken down by country across Asia-Pacific across the available dataset period. This is not a prediction. These are live job advertisements, already published.

Australia's Trajectory
7.6× increase in Australia's share of AI-related job postings, from 0.24% in 2014 to 1.78% in 2025 — from the underlying dataset

This chart is a regional average comparison, not a time series or a 2025-only view. The 7.6× figure comes from the underlying dataset tracking Australia's share over a decade. The chart here places Australia within its regional context — where it sits matters for what comes next.

Asia-Pacific

Singapore and Hong Kong lead the Asia-Pacific comparison, while Australia sits lower but still clearly within the regional AI hiring shift.

In the Asia-Pacific drill-down, Singapore has the highest average AI-related job-posting share at about 2.37%, followed by Hong Kong at about 2.13%. Australia sits below those leaders, at around 0.7% on this average measure, but the underlying time-series data shows its AI-related job-posting share still rose sharply over the decade. The point is not that Australia leads the region; it is that Australia is already moving in the same direction.

What 1.78% Means

By 2025, Australia's AI-related job-posting share reached 1.78% — roughly 1 in 56 job postings — after rising from 0.24% in 2014.

In 2014, Australia's share was 0.24%. By 2025 it is 1.78%. That is not a marginal shift — it is a structural change in what employers expect workers to know. This wider hiring trend sets the context for finance and procurement roles, where AI literacy is becoming part of the skills conversation.

Two Forces of AI

AI reshapes work in two ways at once. Finance sits in the high-exposure zone.

Automation replaces tasks. Augmentation changes them — making human judgment more powerful with AI support. This scatter chart, produced in CODAP from the Jobs and Skills Australia Generative AI Capacity Study, plots approximately 356 occupations on both dimensions simultaneously. The x-axis is automation exposure; the y-axis is augmentation exposure.

Finance in the Scatter
71% of Accounting, Banking & Financial occupations score above 0.5 on both axes — about 7 in 10 roles in this group

Most occupations in the economy sit above the diagonal line where augmentation equals automation — meaning AI augments more than it replaces, economy-wide. Finance is an exception because its automation exposure sits closer to its augmentation exposure than many other fields. That means AI is not only assisting finance work; it is also close to substituting parts of it.

The High-Exposure Zone

Roles scoring above 0.5 on both axes face the steepest combined pressure — and many finance occupations cluster in or near that zone.

In the top-right corner of the scatter, automation is not simply replacing work while AI tools enhance something else. Both forces apply simultaneously. Workers in this zone need to adapt the tasks they perform AND develop new AI-collaboration skills. Many finance occupations cluster in or near this high-exposure zone rather than sitting far from it.

The Extreme Cases

The circled outliers are among the most AI-pressured occupations in the dataset — and finance contributes several.

These circled data points represent occupations where automation and augmentation exposure are both near the top of the scale. Multiple occupations from Accounting, Banking and Financial Services sit in this cluster. This is not a coincidence — it reflects the task-intensive, rules-based nature of much finance work, which is exactly the profile AI processes most readily.

Why It Matters

The scatter confirms that finance is not an average-risk sector — it is a high-risk one on both dimensions. But the scatter does not yet show where within finance the pressure is concentrated. Section 3 confirms that finance leads every other industry group in the JSA data. Section 4 breaks that down by specific role. The key point is that finance is exposed to both sides of AI: support from AI tools and substitution of routine tasks.

The Industry Ranking

Finance is not just one of many sectors at risk. The JSA data ranks it the most automation-exposed of all 23 industry groups.

The Jobs and Skills Australia Generative AI Capacity Study classifies every occupation by industry group and calculates average automation exposure scores. This Power BI chart ranks all 23 industry groups. Accounting, Banking and Financial Services reaches an average automation exposure score of approximately 0.60 — placing it at the top of the distribution.

Above Manufacturing. Above Logistics.

Finance scores higher than manufacturing, transport, and logistics — sectors most people assume are most at risk from automation.

This matters for how Defence understands its finance and procurement workforce. The disruption conversation in government often focuses on physical and industrial work. The data shows the office-based, analytical work inside finance carries equal or greater exposure. Drilling down within the finance group, Accounting Clerks reach an exposure score of approximately 0.71 and Auditors are lowest at approximately 0.45.

Both Directions

The earlier scatter showed finance is also exposed to augmentation — so this is not only a replacement story.

This Power BI graph focuses on automation, but the previous CODAP scatter adds the second dimension: finance is also strongly exposed to augmentation. Together, the two visuals show that finance workers face a dual shift. Some routine tasks are technically substitutable, while the work that remains is likely to become more AI-supported. For Defence finance, this means workers will need to verify AI outputs, supervise AI-assisted analysis, and apply professional judgement.

Role by Role

The divide between junior and senior finance roles is the central finding in the occupation-level data.

This bar chart, produced from the Jobs and Skills Australia Generative AI Capacity Study data in Google Colab, plots eight finance occupations by automation exposure score. The split between red (junior/clerical) and blue (senior/professional) bars tells the core story of where the risk sits.

The Highest Score
71% automation exposure score for Accounting Clerks — the highest of the finance occupations analysed in this data story (score out of 1.0, shown here ×100)

A high automation exposure score of 0.71 means Accounting Clerks carry the greatest concentration of technically substitutable tasks among the selected finance roles analysed here. This is not a statement that 71% of Accounting Clerk jobs will disappear overnight — it is a measure of how much of the work profile is technically within reach of current AI systems.

The Clerical Layer

Accounting Clerks (0.71), Bookkeepers (0.69), Payroll Clerks (0.68), and Credit & Loans Officers (0.68) all score 0.68 or above.

These four roles define the clerical entry level of the finance workforce. They handle high-volume, rules-based transactions — precisely the task profile that AI systems are designed to process.

This pressure is already visible in real hiring decisions. Fortune has reported that major Wall Street firms are weighing cuts to junior analyst hiring of up to two-thirds as banks lean more heavily on AI for the routine analytical work that once defined those roles (Lake, 2025). The task profile driving those cuts is identical to the one the exposure data scores most exposed.

The Professional Layer

Senior roles score lower — but not low. Accountants reach 0.54; Finance Managers and Auditors sit at 0.45–0.46.

A score of 0.54 for Accountants is still high by cross-sector standards. The gap between junior and senior is not a gap between risk and safety. It is a gap between immediate task substitution and more gradual reshaping. Senior finance work requires judgment, accountability and professional discretion — the qualities that survive automation. But they do not confer immunity.

The Core Question

These four high-scoring roles are common early-career pathways into finance. If those roles automate, they do not just disappear — they take their training function with them. Section 5 asks whether that process is becoming a current risk signal. The key point is that junior and clerical finance roles carry the sharpest automation exposure.

Where automation exposure is highest, entry-level job ads are fewest. The pattern is already visible in the data.

The Pipeline Question

The real issue is not just which finance roles AI can perform. It is whether entry-level pathways into finance are under pressure.

Using Jobs and Skills Australia data, this Power BI chart groups finance occupations by their entry-level job advertisement share and automation exposure band. It treats occupation data as a career pathway map, not just a risk inventory. The key question: do the roles with the highest automation exposure also show fewer entry-level job postings?

A Measurable Pattern

Across finance occupations, automation exposure and entry-level job ad share show a moderate negative relationship.

At the occupation level, automation exposure and entry-level job ad share have a moderate negative correlation of approximately −0.56. The grouped Power BI view supports this pattern: the high entry-level share band has lower average automation exposure, while the medium and low entry-level bands carry higher exposure. This matters because the roles most exposed to AI are not necessarily the strongest entry points for young workers.

The Generational Risk

If junior roles automate before young workers have moved up, the profession loses the training ground that makes senior professionals.

The roles most likely to be automated — Accounting Clerks, Bookkeepers, Payroll Clerks — are also the roles that teach young finance workers the fundamentals of the profession. Automating those roles does not just reduce headcount. It removes the learning pathway through which junior workers become senior ones. If the bottom rung disappears, the ladder collapses from below.

Separate supporting evidence comes from a Stanford Digital Economy Lab study analysing payroll records from millions of US workers. It found that early-career workers aged 22 to 25 in the most AI-exposed occupations experienced a 13% relative decline in employment since late 2022, while older workers in the same roles held steady (Stanford Digital Economy Lab, 2025). Critically, those declines were concentrated in roles where AI automates rather than augments the work — the same pattern the exposure data flags for entry-level finance roles. The bottom rung is not just a future concern; the data shows early signs of pressure.

The Adaptation Picture

This final chart compares two pressures — automation exposure and skill-change pressure — across finance role types. They move in opposite directions.

This bar chart, built from Jobs and Skills Australia skill-change data, groups finance occupations into role types and compares two scores for each: automation exposure (how much of the work AI can technically perform) and skill-change pressure (how urgently those roles need to develop new capabilities). The pattern that emerges is the most important finding in the whole data story.

Clerical and Processing Roles

Clerical and processing roles average approximately 0.67 on automation exposure but only around 0.45 on skill-change pressure.

High automation exposure with low skill-change pressure means these roles face the greatest substitution risk while their skill profile is evolving the least rapidly. The work is being automated, and the workers in those roles are not currently being pushed toward the new capabilities that would help them adapt. This is not a reason to abandon these workers — it is a reason to act.

Senior and Management Roles

Senior and management roles average approximately 0.46 on automation exposure but 0.61 on skill-change pressure. The pattern inverts across the career ladder.

Where automation exposure is lower, skill-change pressure is higher. Senior finance and management roles are not being automated away immediately — they are being reshaped. The work that remains increasingly demands judgement, AI output verification, professional accountability, and AI-supported decision-making. Those capabilities are in higher demand at the senior level precisely because AI cannot fully replicate them.

Final Word

The data does not argue that AI will eliminate finance work. It argues something more specific: AI is automating the routine work first, and the routine work is where the profession teaches its next generation. A Defence financial contractor with direct experience of AI in government procurement was direct about this: "you still can't rely 100% on AI — at the end of the day, humans are still responsible for the work and the outcomes AI produces." That accountability — the capacity to direct, verify and own AI-supported decisions — is exactly what the skill-change data shows will be in increasing demand. The pipeline that develops it starts at entry level. Protecting that pipeline is not just a workforce concern. For Defence finance and procurement, it is a professional infrastructure concern. The key point is that junior routine work is more likely to be automated, while senior work is more likely to change and require stronger judgement, verification and AI skills.

The Argument, Summarised

Two datasets. Six views.
One finding that matters
for Defence finance.

AI is not simply replacing finance jobs. It is reshaping finance work unevenly — automating the routine first, transforming the senior roles more slowly. The major risk is not that experienced professionals lose their positions. It is that the entry-level work that trains them may disappear before the next generation has moved through it. That is a pipeline problem — and the data shows it is already measurable.
